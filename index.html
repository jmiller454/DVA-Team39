Introduction

Since 1896 a handful of nations—the United States, Russia/USSR, China, and Germany—have dominated medal tables. We investigate how macro‑level forces (GDP, trade openness, gender mobility, demographics) influence that hegemony.

Research Objectives

  1. Quantify relationships between socio‑economic indicators and medal counts across 28 Summer & Winter Games.
  2. Visualize the impact of geopolitical shocks, host advantages, and resource allocation on performance.
  3. Predict the probability of individual athletes winning medals using open data.

Data & Methods

Data sources: 270 k athlete–event rows from Kaggle’s Olympic history (1896‑2016) were merged with 10 World Bank indicators spanning 1960‑2020.

Feature sets:

  • Athletic – age, sex, height, weight (≈ 151 k rows)
  • Athletic + Economic – athletic features plus GDP, population, Gini, trade openness, host flag, female labour‑force participation, education spend, etc. (≈ 89 k rows)

Pre‑processing: Median imputation, z‑score standardisation, chronological split: training ≤ 2010; validation 2010‑2012; test 2014‑2016.

Modelling: Binary medal prediction with logistic regression, linear SVC, and a 500‑tree class‑balanced random forest.

Related Literature

  • GDP & population: Bernard & Busse (2000) link GDP‑pc to medals; Trivedi & Zimmer (2014) highlight population size.
  • Institutional factors: Kuper & Sterken (2011) associate socialist traditions and host advantages with success.
  • Methodological shifts: Shasha et al. (2022) apply Tobit and quantile models to a single Games; our study spans 28 Games and leverages modern ML.
  • Gap addressed: Trade openness, inequality, and gender mobility are under‑studied; our panel shows their importance.

Predictive Modelling Results

Adding economic variables lifts F‑score from 0.26 to 0.33 (logistic regression). Trade openness, host flag and female labour participation emerge as key predictors; population ranks unexpectedly low.

Socio‑Economic Driver Space

Parallel‑coordinates plotting shows medallists skew toward higher trade openness and lower inequality.

Limitations

  • Strong class imbalance (<10 % positive cases) challenges recall.
  • Pre‑1990 data gaps bias against developing nations.
  • Unobserved cultural and policy variables remain un‑modelled.

Conclusion

Economic capacity matters, but how nations deploy resources—through openness, inclusion and specialization—ultimately determines podium success.
